Abstract.  Correlations between plasma properties and magnetic fluctuations in  the  subsolar magnetosheath  downstream of  a quasi-perpendicular shock have been found and indicate that mirror  and ion  cyclotronlike fluctuations correlate with  the  magnetosheath proper  and  plasma  depletion layer, respectively (Anderson  and Fuselier, 1993). We  explore  the  entire range  of  magnetic  spectral signatures observed  from the  AMPTE/CCE spacecraft in  the  magnetosheath  downstream of  a  quasi-perpendicular shock. ## 1. Introduction

Although magnetosheath flow may  be approximately described by gas  dynamic models,  the  importance of  magnetic forces is  widely  recognized, particularly near  the  magnetopause  in  the  subsolar region  [e.g., Song et  al., 1990b; Wu,  1992].  It  has been theorized  for  some  time that  the subsolar magnetosheath  should  suffer a depletion of  plasma density and an increase in  magnetic  field strength within about 0.5R·. of the magnetopause  [Midgley and Davis,

1963;  Lees,  1964;  Zwan  and  Wolf,  1976; Wu,  1992].  The phenomenon  is  referred to  as  plasma depletion, and the  region of reduced density  and increased  field  strength  is called  the plasma depletion  layer. Observational  evidence has been reported to support  this  expectation [Paschmann et al.,  1978; Crooker  et al.,  1979]  and  has indicated  that the plasma depletion  layer  preferentially forms when  the upstream  shock  is quasi-perpendicular and  when the magnetosheath field is  northward [Anderson and Fuselier, 1993] (hereinafter referred  to  as  AF).

ULF fluctuations in the magnetosheath  magnetic field indicative of both ion cyclotron  and mirror instabilities have  been  found. Fairfield [1976]  first noted  that the magnetosheath near the magnetopause  exhibited  fluctuations  near 1 Hz, below  the local  proton cyclotron  frequency with  a left-hand transverse nature  suggestive of  ion cyclotron  waves.  Tsurutani et al.  [1982] have  observed large-amplitude compressional  fluctuations, 6B/B = 0.5,  in the  magnetosheath that  were attributed to  the  mirror  instability. Subsequent  authors  also  noted magnetic fluctuations characteristic of the  magnetosheath near  the  magnetopause [Song  et al.,  1990a,  1993; Anderson et al.,  1991; AF].

Anderson  et  al.  [1991] and AF  demonstrated a close  association between the  wave  magnetic fluctuations and plasma properties. They  found that  cyclotron waves occurred  for reduced  plasma  density and  increased magnetic  field strength relative to  values  during  mirror  waves. Their  observations  also suggested that  magnetosheath  ion anisotropies may  be ordered  by the  ion  anisotropy mode  that  is most unstable.

Denton et  al.  [1993]  compared  the  magnetic field spectral  observations  for  one ion cyclotron  wave  event with theoretical predictions using  the  observed  plasma properties and showed  that  the  observed  frequency  and polarization structure of  the  magnetic  fluctuations agree  with  theoretical expectations. The solar wind He  2' was found  to  play  a  significant role  in  determining  the  spectral structure. Gary et al.  [1993a]  used measured  plasma data  to show  that  the observed  transition from mirror  to  cyclotron waves occurs where the  growth rates are  comparable and that  the  mode with  the  larger growth rate is  observed  to  be dominant.

We extend the  observational work of  AF to  consider the  full range  of  spectral features observed  downstream of the quasi-perpendicular shock. Since we  use the Active Magnetospheric  Particle Tracer  Explorers/Charge Composition  Explorer  (AMPTE/CCE) data  set, we  necessarily restrict attention to  the  subsolar magnetosheath for  which the magnetosphere  is  compressed.  AF  was primarily concerned with periods  that  were dominated by either compressional fluctuations or left-hand polarized  transverse waves  above 0.5Fp. In  general, a  continuum of  spectral signatures is  observed  between these  extremes.  Characterizing the  variety of spectral signatures and corresponding  plasma conditions is  the  topic of  this paper.

## Progression  of  Spectral  Types

The  variety of spectral types  appearing  in  this  example can be viewed  as a progression  from  compressional to transverse waves.  In  addition, the  spectral types  correspond to  different local plasma conditions: high Bp-low Ap  prevails for  the strongly  compressional fluctuations, whereas low ·v-high Ap  characterizes the  strongest transverse waves. The general inverse correlation of  ·  and  Ap  is apparent in  Plate 1  as  is the correspondence of  spectral type to  ·p  and  Ap.

Finally, the  example bifurcated case  has  a  depression in wave power  extending from about  0.35  to  0.55F·. PL decreases rapidly with  frequency from about  0.9  to  1.0Fp. The left-handed polarization for  F  &gt;  0.5Fp and  the  mixed polarization of  lower  frequencies are  evident. Linear  theory predicts  two  frequency ranges of ion cyclotron  wave growth coincident  with the frequencies  of the spectral peaks.  In this case  the  bands of positive cyclotron wave growth  with  ?/·p &gt; 10 -4  are  separated by  more  than twice their width.

To  provide  a framework  for  quantitative analysis we define  five  spectral categories. The  first category, mirror (MIR), represents instances when  compressional  perturbations  dominate the  spectrum while  there  is  no discernible spectral peak in the transverse power. Note that  the absence  of  a transverse spectral peak does not  mean  that  the transverse power is  negligible. We adopt the  term mirror in  anticipation of the  discussion of wave  modes  presented below. The  second category  consists of intervals with a prominent  compressional peak together with  a  peak at  a  different frequency in  the  transverse spectrum with  F  &lt;  0.5Fp and is  called  MRL for  mirror  with low-frequency  transverse waves. Events  with compressional and transverse peaks at  the same  frequency were considered  to be compressional if  the  compressional  and transverse powers were comparable.  This  situation arose  in  one event. The remaining  categories consist of  intervals with  no discernable peak in  compressional  power  but with  three  different transverse spectral structures: low frequency  (LOW),  transverse waves with  F&lt; 0.5Fv; continuous (CON),  transverse waves  with continuous power  spectra extending above  and  below 0.5F p;  and  bifurcated (B IF), transverse waves with  two broad local maxima  in  spectral power.

The frequency  structure of  the  transverse fluctuations is predicted  by  linear  Vlasov  theory of the ion cyclotron modes.  Since  He  2* in  the  magnetosheath is  hotter than  H* [Fusetier et  at., 1991b;  Gary et  at., 1993c],  we  expect  that He  2*  may  be generating cyclotron waves below 0.5Fp. [1993] shows that He  2*  is  largely responsible for  the  lower  spectral peak and that  protons  drive  waves  with frequencies above  0.5Fp. ## Implications  for  Order  in  the  Magnetosheath

For  the CCE data set,  AF have  shown that transverse fluctuations with  F&gt; 0.5Fp  and left-hand polarization correspond to the plasma  depletion  layer,  whereas the predominantly compressional fluctuations correspond to the magnetosheath  proper.  The  present  analysis therefore implies that the  progression of  plasma and spectral properties corresponds  to spatial structure in  the  magnetosheath.  As plasma  flows along the  Earth-Sun line, f·llP and  Ap  change continuously from  a  high-f·llp/low-A p  near the bow shock  to a  low-f·llp/high-Ap close to  the .magnetopause.

The simulations of  McKean et  al.  [1992]  and Gary and Winske [1993]  are  initial value  computations  that  require relatively large  start-up anisotropies and therefore do not necessarily correspond  to  the  observed  magnetosheath  conditions. The "refreshed" simulations of  Denton  et  al.  [1993] are  probably  a more appropriate way  to  address  the  model parameters,  since  the actual  state  of the plasma  results from a dynamic  equilibrium  between plasma convection, which drives the  anisotropy up,  and the  waves that reduce it. Preliminary results from the initial value  computations provide  reason  for  optimism that  these  aspects of the  observations will  also  find  direct explanation in  terms  of  ion anisotropy instabilities.

## 6. Conclusions

We  have characterized the  magnetic spectral signatures and plasma properties observed  in  the  subsolar magnetosheath  for  quasi-perpendicular upstream shock conditions. The  magnetic spectral signatures and plasma properties were  found  to  progress from  mirror waves  with  high  f·llp and  low  Ap  in  the  magnetosheath proper to  H  +  and  He  2+ cyclotron waves  associated with high Ap  and  low  ·llp in  the plasma depletion layer.

Linear  Vlasov  theory has  been  used  to  test the  interpretation that the  mirror  and cyclotron modes  driven  by the ion temperature  anisotropies are  responsible for  the observed  spectral features [Gary et  al., 1993a;  Denton et  al., 1993]. Linear  theory  correctly predicts which modes should be observed  and also  appears to  predict the  spectral structure  of the  cyclotron  waves. Furthermore,  simulations of polarization and saturation amplitudes  of the  mirror  and cyclotron modes  indicate that  these  features also  arise from the dynamics of these modes [McKean et al.,  1992; Denton  et  al., 1993; Gary  and  Winske, 1993].  We conclude  that the  evolution of spectral structures may  readily be explained by the  generation of  mirror  and cyclotron instabilities in  the  plasma as  it convects  earthward  from the shock  to  the  magnetopause.

The extent  to  which the  magnetosheath plasma evolves toward the low-f·llp/high-Ap extreme depends on  the orientation of  the  magnetosheath field, lower  ·llp occurring for northward field  [AF].  It  is  natural  to  ascribe  the  difference to  reconnection such  that the  additional flow diversion provided  by reconnection relieves some of the  burden for  diverting the  magnetosheath  plasma.

## Remaining  Theoretical  Challenges

The agreement between observed spectral structure and predicted frequency  ranges  of  positive growth presented  in Figure 1 is  tantalizing. It  is  important  to  assess  how  well the  linear Vlasov theory  predicts the  evolution of spectral structures more generally, perhaps using the  Ap-/·llp relation. If  linear theory  continues  to  be successful, this  will substantiate the  mode identifications and the  conclusion that  the  waves are  generated  locally. This  theoretical study is  under  way.

Theoretical studies of the  fluctuation amplitudes, polarization structure, and further relationships among  the  plasma parameters for  comparison  with  the  observations are  of  considerable  interest but  are  more difficult.  As Denton et al. [1993]  have discussed, treatment  of these  issues  requires that  nonlinear saturation effects  be included,  which  in turn implies  that  attention must be paid to the  timescales  of wave  growth and residence  with resonant  particles. Hence quantitative predictions concerning  spectral amplitudes  and plasma  responses to instability growth  may  come  only from fully nonlinear computer simulations.

Additional evidence  that  the  compressional fluctuations result from the mirror  mode  is  provided by correlation analysis of  density and field fluctuations. Density  and field correlations show both that  the  field and density perturbations  are  in  antiphase, and that  the  compressibility C· is small, 0.13  on average, consistent with  the  expectation that C·  &lt; 1  for  mirror  waves.

The widespread  generation of ion  anisotropy modes  in the  subsolar magnetosheath  plasma implies that  wave-particle interactions play  a role  in  determining the  ion  anisotropy  state of  the  plasma.  This  is  clearly indicated by Figure  6.  Since  the  ions  carry  the  majority of  plasma  pressure in  the  magnetosheath, any process  that  regulates the  distribution  of ion  pressures  may  be significant for  magnetosheath  dynamics.  To  accurately model the  magnetosheath, proper account of the effects of the locally  generated anisotropy instabilities needs  to  be made. We  suggest  that this include wave-particle ffects using  the  relationship between  Ap and /·llp as  an the  upper  limit on the  proton anisotropy.
