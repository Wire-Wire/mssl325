We  have analyzed  22 AMPTE/IRM satellite passes  through  the  low-latitude magnetopause region  for which the  magnetic shear, i.e.,the field rotation angle  on  transit from the  magnetosheath to  the  magnetosphere, was less than 30  ø  .  We  found that  on all passes  a  key  time  could  be  identified where the proton temperature, and usually  also  in  the  electron  temperature  and the  temperature anisotropies, show  net and often  discontinuous  changes. A  change in  plasma  flow  direction  also occurs at this  time.  The  small field rotations  that occur have no fixed  relationship  to the key time. Earthward  of  the  key time,  the  plasma has  the  characteristics of  the  magnetopause boundary layer, i.e., reduced density  and bulk velocity, and in  particular an electron  temperature  anisotropy with Tell · Tea, until further and usually more dramatic  changes  in  plasma thermal  properties mark  the entry into  the magnetosphere proper.  Boundary layer  durations varied  widely,  from 4  s  to 14min.  On  the  magnetosheath side  we observed  a layer of  plasma density  depletion  (by a  factor ·f  two or  more) and  concurrent magnetic field pile-up in less than half the crossings. This depletion  layer  was 3  min wide on average  and often  characterized by a drop in  Tell, as predicted.  From  the observational  evidence  we conclude that  the changes in  plasma  thermal and flow  properties  at  the key time mark  the crossing of  the  magnetopause  under conditions  of  low magnetic  shear.  It  is tempting  to  attribute these  changes  to  the  crossing of  a  topological boundary, e.g., a transition from open interplanetary to closed  geomagnetic  field lines.  There is  evidence, however, that in  some  cases  the field lines  immediately  earthward  of  the key time are  not closed. The  consistent  presence of  a boundary  layer  inside  the low-shear  magnetopause confirms earlier inferences  that solar  wind  plasma  can enter the magnetosphere regardless  of  field orientation. Inspection of  the velocity  distribution functions  indicates  that the plasma  is  heated upon  entry. Taking measured  plasma velocities along  the  magnetopause normal direction, we have inferred  an average  magnetopause  speed of  11  km/s, an indication that  the  low-shear  magnetopause  moves more  slowly.  The  duration of  the temperature  transition translates into  a thickness  of  often  less than 50  km, comparable with the  typical p·'oton gyroradius.  For  the  plasma depletion layer  and the boundary  layer  we obtained average thicknesses  of  0.4  and 0.3  Rr,  respectively.

## 1. INTRODUCTION

The  properties of  the Earth's  magnetopause  under conditions  of large  magnetic shear have been  studied  extensively since  the  first in  situ  measurements  in 1961.  The identification of  the  magnetopause  is usually straightforward in  these  cases  because  it  can be based on the  large  rotation, i.e., large shear, displayed by the magnetic field as  the  observing  spacecraft passes  from one side  of  the  magnetopause  to  the  other.  Large shear at the subsolar  magnetopause  implies  southward  directed interplanetary magnetic  field (IMF).  For  northward IMF,  on the  other  hand, it  can happen  that  the fields on the  two sides of  the  dayside  magnetopause become (nearly) parallel to  each  other, i.e., exhibit low shear.  Under these  conditions the  identification  of the magnetopause from magnetometer  data alone  becomes difficult or  impossible, particularly if one  notes  that  the field magnitude often  changes  little as  well. Analysis  of magnetopause crossings with  large magnetic  shear  has to a large  extent  focussed  on the search  for  evidence of  magnetic reconnection  across  the magnetopause, a process  favored  by large  field rotation  angles. Lowshear  cases, on the  other  hand, are  of  interest because

A survey of  the  AMPTE/IRM dayside data  revealed a number  of  passes  where according  to  the  measured plasma density  profiles a transition from the magnetosheath  to  the  magnetosphere must have occurred  at some time,  but the  magnetic field changed very  little. However, here  one is  faced  with a dilemma.  The boundary  layer  is  located  on terrestrial field lines, and therefore the  field orientation will not  vary  much  on entry  into  or  exit  from it. Therefore  a distinction between multiple  low-shear  magnetopause crossings and encounters  of  an isolated boundary layer  is  not possible  a priori.  Some  independent signature  of the boundary  layer  is  required  to make that  distinction. As we will  see,  the boundary  layer  is usually  characterized  by positive electron  temperature anisotropies, while  in  the  magnetosheath the  anisotropy is  usually  slightly less or  equal  to  zero.  Note again  that the  anisotropy parameter has  been  defined as  Tii/T·\_-1. Thus Tell &gt; Te·\_ in  the  boundary  layer. Using  this criterion, the interval after  10:55:30UT  would indeed be classified as the boundary  layer, and not as a pair  of low-shear  crossings of  the  magnetopause.

## 2.2. Survey Results

The survey  of  all AMPTE/IRM crossings in  the  0816 GSM local time range  has  produced  a total of  22 passes where a magnetopause with  less than 30 ø  shear could be identified.  The  location  of the crossings  is shown  in  Figure 1. It  is  likely that  some  passes  involved multiple  low-shear  crossings.  But  for  reasons discussed  earlier, only  the  outermost transition unquestionably  marks a magnetopause crossing. Any  other low-shear magnetopause transitions further Earthward on the same pass  could  not a priori be distinguished from passes into  and out  of  the  boundary layer.

The  situation becomes less  clear  if in a series  of  multiple magnetopause  crossings the outermost ones are high-shear crossings, which are  followed by crossings which have become  low-shear  as  a result of  a  temporal change  in  the  direction of  the  IMF. That often  can  be  determined  rather reliably from the  fluctuation  levels in  the  magnetic  field. In  other  cases  counting  the  number of  crossings can  uniquely clarify the  situation if  one notes that there must  be an odd  total  number  of  magnetopause  crossings in  a  given  pass, or  that after any even number of  crossings one has returned

## 3. SUPERPOSED EPOCH ANALYSIS

In the  previous  section  we have established that  the low-shear  magnetopause  crossings are  associated  with sudden net changes in  plasma thermal properties. We also found certain systematic  variations in  other  plasma and  magnetic field quantities which followed  or preceded the thermal changes in  a number  of  cases.  To study these  relationships more systematically, we have performed  a superposed epoch  analysis  of the crossings,  using  the  times  of  the  proton temperature jump as  the key time. For ease  of  reference, we will  refer to the  key times  as  the  magnetopause  crossing. We  have excluded from the analysis  cases  14 and  20. The  former  because no electron  data exist, the latter because the proton temperature was the only quantity  which showed a change.

We  used printouts  of  the data to precisely identify the  times  (Table  1).  Three minutes  of  data  were  included  on  either side of  the  key  time,  unless other  magnetopause  or  boundary layer crossings occurred  within the  3 min. In those  cases  shorter  intervals  were  chosen. For  outbound crossings, the  time  order of  the  data  was reversed. As the  absolute magnitude of  some quantities varies  considerably  from one case  to  the next,  we normalized their  averages  over  the  entire time interval to 1.  The normalized quantities for  the  20  cases  were then added together  logarithmically, using 10-s  time bins. Logarithmic averaging  reduces  the  dominance  of  cases with larger  than average  dynamic  range. With  4.4s spacing  of  the measurements, between two and three data points  are  binned together.  For each bin,  the  error  of  the  mean  was also  computed.

For the  temperature anisotropies the absolute  value matters, and normalization is thus  ruled out.  As signed quantities they also  had to  be summed linearly. This does not cause  problems because  the  anisotropies have little dynamic  range.

The magnetic  field direction in  the  plane  tangent  to to  the  (model)  magnetopause,  ·B,  was  also treated differently. As the L axis  of  the boundary  normal coordinate  system is  close to  the  field direction in  the  subsolar  magnetosphere, TB  is  automatically  small  there, but not  exactly  zero.  We  therefore normalized the  data such that  for  each case  the average  on the magnetosphere  side  of  the  key time  is  precisely zero.  As we are interested only  in  the  magnitude of  the  field rotation, we changed the  sign  of  the  angle  on the  magnetosheath side  whenever its average  there  was negative.  The  20 cases  were then averaged  linearly.

The plasma flow  direction Tv, which we included  in the  superposed epoch analysis even though it  was not shown  in the sample cases,  was treated  in much  the same way as  ·. Here we normalized  each  case  to  zero on the  sheath  side  and switched  sign  on the  magnetospheric side, if not  already  positive. This  was to  account for  the  facts that  Tv  has no natural  reference value  in the  magnetosphere and is  determined more accurately in  the  magnetosheath.

Figures  8-10 show the  results of  the  analysis. Figure 8 exhibits the  ion  and electron  thermal properties whose changes  defined  the  key times.  It  is  thus  by design  that  these  four  quantities have their  changes centered  at  the  key  time. ## 5. DISCUSSION

## 5.1. Magnetopause  Definition and Identification

The  simplest  definition considers the  magnetopause as  the  region of  current flow  in  the  transition between the  magnetosheath and the  magnetosphere. In  this  paper we have been considering conditions of  low magnetic shear  across the  magnetopause, and thus  are  dealing  with  cases where little or  no  current is needed  to  separate  magnetosheath from magnetospheric  field lines, at  least locally. In  this  situation the  above operational definition is  not helpful.  Worse, this  definition would subsume  under "magnetopause" the  region  we observe in  about half  the  cases  where current flows  outside the  magnetopause because  the  interplanetary magnetic field is  piled  up against the  magnetospheric  obstacle.

In  this dilemma  we turn  to  the  observed changes in the plasma properties.  If  the magnetopause  were an impenetrable  boundary separating the  solar wind from the magnetospheric  plasma, the magnetopause  could be easily. identified, regardless of  any magnetic  field changes, because  the  two plasmas  happen to  have  distinctly different densities and temperatures.  The magnetopause  is, however,  not  impenetrable, as  evidenced by the  usual  presence of  a  boundary layer. Because  of the  boundary layer, it  is  not a priori clear what signatures  to  expect  in  the  plasma properties which could  be used to  identify the  magnetopause.

We  have demonstrated in  this paper that,  in  spite of the  boundary  layer, the  magnetopause  transition is  not smooth but characterized by a  distinct jump  in  plasma temperatures  and temperature  anisotropies, as  well  as an abrupt change in  flow  direction. It  is  tempting to attribute these  changes,  and in  particular their  often discontinuous nature,  to  the  crossing of  a  boundary between topologically different magnetic fields, i.e., between closed  magnetospheric  and open interplanetary field lines.

There are,  however,  difficulties with that  interpretation. As pointed  out  below,  there is evidence that  in  2  of the  22  cases there  are  open field lines Earthward of  the temperature boundary,  which would rule  out  the  topological explanation at  least for those two  cases. There  is also the  fact, already  noted  earlier, that  the  small  magnetic  field rotations we observe  have no fixed  temporal relation to  the  key time where the changes in  plasma properties occur.  If a  spatial interpretation applies, this would mean that the current flow associated  with  the field rotation has  no  fixed spatial relation to  the  plasma boundary.

Thus  it  appears that while  the changes in plasma properties observed  at  the  key  time  constitute a  reliable signature of  the  magnetopause crossing for  conditions of  low  magnetic  shear, the  interpretation of  this plasma boundary,  as  well  as  the  complete  structure and width of  the  magnetopause,  remain  to  be  clarified.

## 5.2. Magnetopause  Thickness  and Speed

To  translate  the noted magnetopause  time scales into  spatial scales requires knowledge of  the  speed  of the boundary  relative to the spacecraft.  With  a single  spacecraft that  information is  not  readily available. One  possibility is  to use the measured  instantaneous plasma  velocity  component along the magnetopause normal to  deconvolve  the  parameter profiles and to  determine  the  thickness, as  done recently for  the  December  29,  1984, crossing [Paschmann et  al., 1990].

Taking a simpler  approach,  we have computed  the average measured  plasma velocity along model  magnetopause normals for  a 40s interval centered  on the magnetopause times  for  each  of  the  22  passes  included in  this study.  The normal speeds  thus  obtained  ranged from  3  to 71  kin/s, with  an  arithmetic mean of  15  km/s and a standard  deviation of  14km/s.  The geometric mean  is  11km/s. In 3 of  the 22 cases  the sign  of  the average  normal velocity differed from the  sign  inferred from the  sense  of the  crossing (inbound  versus  outbound). Noting that we employed but model magnetopause  normals this  is probably not  surprising.

For comparison,  the  timing  analysis of  a  set of  ISEE 1/2  crossings containing all shears  [Berchem  and  Russell, 1982] has  provided an  average magnetopause  speed of  40  km/s.  No distinction according to  magnetic  shear was  made in that study. Supporting evidence for smaller  speeds  for low-shear  cases  can be  inferred, however, from  another ISEE study  [Song et  al., 1988] where it  was found  that  the  amplitude  of  magnetopause motion is  less  for  northward than  for  southward IMF. If  the periods of  the  oscillations were  nearly identical for the two field directions, then the velocity must  be smaller for the  northward case.

As noted earlier, the  temperature transition is  often less  than 4s wide.  Using our average  magnetopause speeds,  4s translate into  60  km  or less.  For comparison,  a proton of  average  thermal speed in the average  magnetopause  magnetic  field has  a  gyro  radius of 60  kin.  As noted above,  the  temperature transition is perhaps  embedded  in  a  wider  magnetopause structure, whose duration and width we have not been able  to determine.

Using  the  the  magnetopause normal velocities inferred earlier (section 5.2), we have  computed the  thicknesses of  the  10  individual cases. Accepting  the  dashed  line in  Figures 8-10  as  marking the  outer  edge of  the  magnetopause, the  gradual  drop in  density  preceding  this  time constitutes a plasma depletion layer. Figure  10  demonstrates  the  existence of  a depletion  layer only  on average.  The superposed epoch technique  also  smears out the  duration  of  the  feature. Inspection  of  the density  profiles of  all  passes  reveals that  in 10 of the  22  cases  a monotonic decrease in density, by a  factor of  two or  more,  is observed.  Using  less rigorous selection criteria would probably  increase the percentage  of  depletion layer  cases.

## 5.4. Magnetic Field  Overshoot

In  some of  the  individual crossings, and still visible in the superposed epoch results, overshoots  in magnetic field strength  right at  the  magnetopause, or  over the  entire boundary  layer  were observed.  The  appearance  of  such  maxima  just  inside the  magnetopause was noted  long ago  in  OGO  5  data  [Neugebauer et  al., 1974] and stressed again  recently in  the  papers  by Hall  et  al. [1990, 1991] which  are based  on  AMPTE/UKS data.

A plasma depletion layer was originally predicted by Midgley and  Davis  [1963] and  Lees  [1964], and  treated in  detail by Zwan and Wolf [1976]. Observational evidence for  the  occasional occurrence  of  a  depletion  layer was reported by Paschmann  et  al. [1978], Crooker  et al. [1979], Song  et  al. [1990b], and  Fuselief et  al. [1991]. Hall  et  al. [1990, 1991] also consider plasma  depletion but in  a different flamework, as  discussed  below.

The low-shear  magnetopause  crossing discussed  earlier by Song et  al. [1990a] may  have  contained a depletion  layer, although these  authors interpreted  the broad region  of  density  drop and field increase they  observe  as  part  of  the  magnetopause  itself and called it the "sheath  transition layer".  Their Figure la shows that  at  the  Earthward edge  of  this layer  the  ion  temperature  increases slightly, perhaps  a  sign  that  that  is where according  to  our  definition the  magnetopause was crossed.

One of  the  predicted signatures of  the  plasma depletion  layer  is  a drop in  temperature  anisotropy,  i.e., a gain  in  perpendicular  over  parallel temperature,  resulting  from the  combined effects of  field compression  and

Hall  et  al. [1990, 1991] observe a  field maximum  and concurrent  minimum in  electron pressure  Earthward  of the magnetopause in  a number  of  cases. They  suggest  that  the  field compression  and plasma depletion is  generated  when  the  field lines are  draped around the region of  closed field lines, rather than  around the  magnetopause  (which  they  locate further outward). In a recent paper, $onnerup  et  al. [1992] have  demonstrated that  the  maximum in B and concurrent minimum in electron (and ion)  pressures can be accounted  for  by mechanisms  not invoking  field pile-up and plasma depletion, having  to  do  with  field curvature and/or  energetic  particle pressure.

## 5.6. Magnetopause  Processes

For the conditions  we  have studied  in this  paper, where the  magnetic fields on the  two sides of  the  lowlatitude magnetopause  are  nearly  parallel, one expects magnetic reconnection  in the subsolar  region  not to play  a role.  Thus  the  low-shear  data set  ought to  be particularly useful for studying  non-reconnection transfer  processes. One  would  then tend to take the fact that  we  are  observing  a boundary  layer  in  all  crossings  as  proof  that  non-reconnection transfer processes are  indeed  occurring, and that  these  processes are  acting  locally. However, neither conclusion is  safe.  First, there  is  no assurance  that  the  plasma entered  locally, it  could  equally  well  have entered  somewhere  else  and been transported  to  the  our observation  site at  a later time.  Second, reconnection  at a remote site, in particular the polar  cusps,  could  provide  boundary  layer plasma  [e.g., Cowley,  1981;  Song and Russell, 1992]. In fact, it  has been suggested  that  the cases  referred to above where we  have  found evidence for finite  normal magnetic  fields for  two  of  our  low-shear cases could be interpreted in  exactly that  way [Paschmann et  al., 19901 .

As to  the  role of  non-reconnection  processes, important  information  should  be contained  in  the  parameter profiles across  the boundary  layer.  For example, one would hope to  infer the  presence  or  absence  of  diffusion from the character  of  the  density  profiles. This would require knowledge  of  the  true  spatial profiles, which as noted above is  not  readily available.

An  important finding of  our study with regard  to the  plasma  entry processes concerns the  nature of  the temperature  changes  observed at  the  magnetopause.  In principle, these  could  be caused  either by bulk  heating of  the  plasma upon entry  from the  magnetosheath into the boundary  layer, or as  a result of  the  observed admixture  of  ho·  magnetospheric plasma.  In  section 4  we have  demonstrated that  the  bulk  plasma itself is hotter in  the boundary  layer.  Unless  one is  willing to postulate that  the  entry  is  always  occurring at  a remote place  where the  magnetosheath plasma happens to  be hotter  than at  our  observation site, this finding means that  the  plasma  is  truly heated  upon entry. By what mechanism  remains  to  be explained.

## 6. SUMMARY

In this  paper we have established a method, based on  changes  in  plasma  thermal  properties, to  identify the magnetopause  under  conditions of low  (\_· 30 ø)  magnetic shear. This temperature boundary occurs  simultaneously  with  a  plasma  flow boundary.  It  is tempting  to attribute both to  the  crossing of  a  topological boundary,  but that interpretation seems to be inconsistent with  independent deductions on  field topology at  least in  two cases. It  also appears  that  the  narrow  temperature  transition is  embedded  in  a  wider  magnetopause current structure.

As mentioned earlier, the  boundary  layer  can be distinguished in  almost  all cases  from the  magnetosheath by the  electron temperature anisotropy. While for  the sheath the  anisotropy  is  smaller  than or  equal  to  zero, in the boundary  layer  it  becomes  nearly  always positive (i.e., Tii  &gt;  Tñ),  0.2  on average.  This  feature greatly helps  to  identify the  boundary layer.  By contrast, the ion  temperature anisotropy, even though it usually  changes  by a larger amount  than that  of  the electrons, remains less  than zero  on either  side  of  the boundary, and therefore is  of  much  less help  in  differentiating the regions.
