## Depletion of  Solar Wind Plasma  Near a  Planetary Boundary

B. J.  ZWAN ·  AND R.  A.  WOLF

Department  of  Space  Physics and  Astronomy,  Rice  University, Houston,  Texas 77001

A  mathematical model  is  presented  that  describes  the squeezing of solar  wind  plasma  out along interplanetary magnetic field lines  in the region  between the  bow  shock and the  effective planetary boundary  (in  the  case  of  the  earth, the  magnetopause). In  the  absence  of  local  magnetic merging the squeezing  process  should  create a 'depletion layer,' a region  of  very  low plasma density just  outside the magnetopause. Numerical solutions are  obtained  for  the  dimensionless magnetohydrodynamic equations describing this depletion  process  for  the  case  where the  solar wind magnetic  field is perpendicular  to  the solar wind flow  direction. For the  case  of  the  earth  with  a  magnetopause  standoff distance of  10  Rs,  the theory  predicts that  the  density should  be  reduced  by a  factor &gt;2 in  a  layer about  700-1300 km  thick  if MA, the  Alfv6n Mach  number  in  the  solar wind,  is equal  to  8.  The layer thickness should  vary  as  MA -·' and should  be approximately  uniform for  a large area  of  the  magnetopause  around the  subsolar  point. Our computed  layer  thicknesses are  somewhat smaller  than those  derived  from Lees'  axisymmetric model. Depletion  layers should  develop  fully only  where magnetic  merging is locally unimportant.  Scaling of  the model calculations to  Venus and Mars suggests layer thicknesses about  ½· and · those  of  the  earth, respectively, neglecting diffusion and ionospheric  effects.

## INTRODUCTION

It  is well  known  that  many  plasma flow  features of  the  solar wind-geomagnetic  interaction can be obtained from the  continuum  equations  of  magnetohydrodynamics. It  has been established [Spreiter et  al., 1966;  Spreiter and  Alksne,  1968,  1969] that  simple  gasdynamics is  sufficient to  reproduce  the  broad characteristics of  plasma flow  around the  magnetosphere.

Starting from the  gasdynamic flow  fields computed by Spreiter  and  co-workers,  Alksne [1967]  used the assumption of frozen-in  flux  (E +  v  x  B/c  =  0) to derive  magnetic field patterns  in  the  magnetosheath for  several  assumed orientations of  the  magnetic field in  the  upstream solar  wind. The present  work constitutes an extension and an elaboration  of  the  paper by Lees [1964], who  considered  magnetic stress and obtained  a magnetohydrodynamic solution  for  the solar wind-geomagnetic interaction for  a  restricted region, the stagnation streamline. In  this paper we apply  the  MHD approach to  plasma flow near  the  magnetopause,  performing  the  calculation for  regions off  the  stagnation  streamline  and for  nonaxisymmetric flow. We  also  examine the  dependence of  our results on solar  wind parameters.  The  treatment  is  aimed primarily  at  the  earth's magnetosheath, but scaling to  other  cases  (particularly Mars and Venus) is  discussed.

## FORMULATION OF  THE MODEL

Our depletion model describes the  process whereby portions of  interplanetary flux  tubes, portions  that  lie in  the  magnetosheath near the  day side  magnetopause,  become  depleted  of plasma. By  depleted  we  mean that  the density  and the  gas pressure drop well  below the  values  achieved  just  after passage through the bow  shock, while the total  pressure  (gas and magnetic)  does  not  dr'op below its postshock  value. Two processes deplete the  tubes. By deflecting plasma around the magnetosphere  the bow shock  kicks plasma out  along  the  field lines away from the  nose  of  the  magnetosphere,  and the  compressional stress exerted on magnetosheath flux tubes  near  the nose  tends  to  squeeze  plasma out  along  the  field lines, further depleting them. There are  two reference frames. The xyz system is  curvilinear and time  dependent; its y axis is everywhere parallel to  the  magnetosheath magnetic  field, and field lines are  curves of  constant x and z. The xyz system E  x  B drifts with the  plasma, allowing  us  to follow  the motion  of a tube of magnetic flux  through the magnetosheath. The y = 0 surface coincides with  the  Y = 0 plane,  which contains  the  earth  and is  perpendicular to  Bsw. The y coordinates gives the  distance from the  Y = 0  plane  as measured along a field line. It  never  becomes  necessary  to define the  x and z  coordinates  completely.

The plasma density  and the  cross-sectional area  of  the  flux tubes decrease  as depletion  time increases. The  ratio  of the density  at  time  zero  to  the  density at  some  depletion time  t  is always greater  than one,  and we call this  ratio the  depletion factor, since  it is  a measure of  the  amount  of  depletion  fo·  a given  depletion  time.  Our  method  of  computing  the  depletion factor is based  on  the fact that one  can  get a  useful approximate  solution  for flow within a flux  tube without knowing  in  detail the  trajectory of  the  tube  through the  magnetosheath.  Our  approach  is  therefore  the following: (1)  to calculate the  motion of  plasma within  a given  flux  tube  thus computing  the  density  p(y,  t)  and magnetic field B(y,  t), (2) to calculate  the motion of the intersection  of the flux tube  with the  X-Z  plane  (Y = y = 0  plane)  perpendicular to  the  magnetic field, specifically, to compute  as a function  of time,  the  distances  of  the  flux  tube from the  magnetopause  and from the stagnation  streamline as  measured  in  the  X-Z  plane, and (3)  to combine  the  results of  the  previous  two calculations to  obtain p and B as  functions of  distance from the  magnetopause  for  a given  region  of  the  magnetopause.

As the  flux tube  is compressed, the  plasma  inside will tend  to squeeze toward  the  ends. Relationship to  obsercations. Ina  magnetom?ter  ecord a depletion layer  is  indicated if  the  magnitude of  the  field is approximately the  same on the  sunward and earthward  sides of the magnetopause  as defined  by an abrupt field angle change.  If the  magnetopause is approximated by a tangential discontinuity and if the  field magnitude is constant  across the discontinuity, the  particle pressure  on both sides should  be approximately the  same thus  yielding an anomalously  high field strength in  the  magnetosheath  adjacent to  the  magnetopause.  We  define 'anomalously  low or high' with  respect to what one  would normally  expect for  compressed  solar wind.  A rough survey  of  published magnetometer data  from magnetopause  crossings [Zwan,  1973]  suggests that the  depletion layer signature occurs  quite commonly  on the  order  of half  the crossings.

Of course, a depletion layer can be identified reliably in  a single satellite crossing only  if simultaneous plasma and magnetic field data  are  available. There are  relatively few  published records of such simultaneous data during a magnetopause crossing. One  case  that  has  been  exhaustively studied is  the ATS 1  magnetopause encounter  of  January 13-14,  1967;  in that case, Cummings  and  Coleman [1968] saw a  clear depletion layer signature in  the  magnetic  field, and Freeman et  al. [1968] observed  an anomalously low plasma density in  the  magnetosheath  just outside  the  magnetopause.

## APPENDIX C:  MAGNETIC TENSION ANALYSIS

Inclusion of  magnetic tension in  the  analysis hould  decrease the  layer thickness. Tension  in  the  magnetic  field lines is expected  to accelerate flux  tubes  essentially parallel to the magnetopause boundary away from the  X axis  and the  subsolar point. Streamlines that  intersect he  bow  shock  close to the equatorial p ane converge toward the magnetopause. Thus the  additional acceleration imposed by  the  inclusion of  tension results in  a flux tube for  a given  convection  time  being  convected  farther along  a  given  streamline and therefore closer to the magnetopause.
