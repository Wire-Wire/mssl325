## Magnetosheath plasma stability and ULF wave occurrence as a function of location in the magnetosheath and upstream bow shock parameters

Jan Soucek 1 , C. Philippe Escoubet 2 , and Benjamin Grison 1

Institute of Atmospheric Physics, Academy of Sciences of the Czech Republic, Prague, Czech Republic, 2 ESTEC, European Space Agency, Noordwijk, Netherlands

Abstract Wepresent the results of a statistical study of the distribution of mirror and Alfvén-ion cyclotron (AIC) waves in the magnetosheath together with plasma parameters important for the stability of ULF waves, specifically ion temperature anisotropy and ion beta. Magnetosheath crossings registered by Cluster spacecraft over the course of 2 years served as a basis for the statistics. For each observation we used bow shock, magnetopause, and magnetosheath flow models to identify the relative position of the spacecraft with respect to magnetosheath boundaries and local properties of the upstream shock crossing. A strong dependence of both plasma parameters and mirror/AIC wave occurrence on upstream Θ Bn and MA is identified. We analyzed a joint dependence of the same parameters on Θ Bn and fractional distance between shock and magnetopause, zenith angle, and length of the flow line. Finally, the occurrence of mirror and AIC modes was compared against the respective instability thresholds. We noted that AIC waves occurred nearly exclusively under mirror stable conditions. This is interpreted in terms of different characters of nonlinear saturation of the two modes.

## 1. Introduction

In this article we address the problem of spatial distribution of ultralow frequency (ULF) waves and plasma parameters determining the stability of plasma in the terrestrial magnetosheath. Based on a large statistical data set, we studied the relative occurrence of the waves in relation to the parameters of the upstream shock crossing and the location of the observation within the magnetosheath.

Already, early satellite observations have shown that the magnetosheath is populated by intense magnetic field fluctuations [ Fairfield and Ness , 1970] at time scales from 1 s to tens of seconds and explanations of their origin in terms of local plasma instabilities were proposed already in that era [ Kaufmann et al. , 1970; Fairfield , 1976]. The ion instabilities responsible for these oscillations are driven by ion temperature anisotropy A = T ⊥ ∕ T ‖ &gt; 1 in high ion beta plasma /u1D6FD ‖ = 2 /u1D707 0 nkT ‖ ∕ | B | 2 &gt; 1, conditions typical for the magnetosheath [ Sckopke etal. , 1990; Thomsenetal. , 1985]. Here n denotesplasmaiondensity, B themagneticfieldvector,andsubscripts ‖ and ⊥ correspond to components parallel and perpendicular to local B .

Later research based primarily on data from ISEE and AMPTE satellites has shown that two wave modes constitute a large majority of magnetosheath ULF waves: the mirror mode waves and kinetic Alfvén-ion cyclotron (AIC) waves, also named electromagnetic ion cyclotron waves in the literature [ Anderson and Fuselier , 1993; Anderson et al. , 1994; Denton et al. , 1994; Song et al. , 1994; Hubert et al. , 1989, 1998; Lacombe et al. , 1992]. For a more recent review of magnetosheath wave modes, see Schwartz et al. [1996].

Thespatial distribution of the ULF waves in the magnetosheath has also been studied previously. It was shown that the AIC waves are found predominantly near the bow shock [ Song et al. , 1994; Hubert et al. , 1998] and close to the magnetopause and, in particular, in the plasma depletion layer [ Anderson and Fuselier , 1993], while mirror structures dominate in the central and downstream magnetosheath [ Génot et al. , 2009a; Verigin et al. , 2006] but can occur immediately downstream from the shock too [ Hubert et al. , 1989]. The above studies also showed that the ULF fluctuations are generally stronger in the dayside magnetosheath. Anderson and Fuselier [1993] performed a small-scale statistical study of the dependence of the observed ULF waves on the upstream shock type. They distinguished between quasi-perpendicular (Q ⊥ ) and quasi-parallel (Q ‖ ) magnetosheath based on a flux of locally observed energetic He ++ ions which is known to be stronger behind Q ‖ shock. This coarse technique allowed to establish that mostly AIC waves were observed in Q ‖ magnetosheath, while in the Q ⊥ magnetosheath the ratio of mirror and AIC observations was close to 1. In their data set, the occurrence rate of either type of wave was lower in Q ‖ magnetosheath.

Themirror structures have received considerable attention recently, when their nonlinear behavior and shape have been addressed. Mirror structures are known to appear in several forms: mirror peaks, dips, and sometimes more irregular or quasi-sinusoidal structures. Several statistical and numerical studies described the relative occurrence of peaks and dips as a function of local plasma parameters and location within magnetosheath [ Soucek et al. , 2008; Califano et al. , 2008; Génot et al. , 2009b, 2011]. It was shown that mirror peaks only appear in mirror unstable plasma ( R &gt; 1), most often in central dayside magnetosheath, while dips can exist for even lower values of R and dominate in low/u1D6FD plasma, such as in the magnetosheath flanks and close to the magnetopause.

The character of locally generated ULF waves is determined by local plasma properties which are largely affected by the process of heating and compression of solar wind plasma taking place at the bow shock. From Rankine-Hugoniot relations [ Chao et al. , 1995; Génot , 2009] it can be shown that downstream temperature anisotropy and /u1D6FD are determined by solar wind parameters and the local properties of the shock: the angle Θ Bn between the shock normal and upstream interplanetary magnetic field (IMF) and local shock Mach number (in this work, we used Alfvénic Mach number MA ). This is true for magnetosheath plasma near the bow shock; further downstream in the magnetosheath, other effects such as expansions or instabilities affect the plasma conditions.

This dependence naturally results in an asymmetric distribution of plasma parameters and waves in the magnetosheath driven by the IMF direction and can be identified in statistical studies as dawn-dusk asymmetry due to the typical Parker spiral orientation of IMF. A number of studies summarized, for example, in a recent review article by Walsh et al. [2014] have shown dawn-dusk asymmetries in plasma density, velocity, magnetic field magnitude, and in the nature of magnetic field fluctuations. The dawn-dusk asymmetry dependent on the IMF orientation is conveniently characterized in the magnetosheath interplanetary medium (MIPM) reference frame [ Verigin et al. , 2006], where the dawnside is always connected to a Q ‖ shock and duskside to Q ⊥ .

In the MIPM frame Dimmock and Nykyri [2013] showed that the dawn magnetosheath contains stronger magnetic field and faster plasma flow, but no significant asymmetry in density was found in contrast to other studies such as Paularena et al. [2001] and Walsh et al. [2012]. Statistical studies have shown that the overall level of magnetic field oscillations is larger on the Q ‖ sides of the magnetosheath [ Luhmann et al. , 1986; Dimmocketal. , 2014] and that there is a qualitative difference in the nature of oscillations [ Shevyrevetal. , 2007; Petrinec , 2013]. On the Q ‖ side, the field direction fluctuates on the time scale of minutes, corresponding to turbulence originating from the Q ‖ shock and possibly foreshock. The Q ⊥ side is populated to a larger extent by smaller-scale oscillation of compressional nature, corresponding to locally generated waves.

## 2. Data Set and Data Processing

The present work is based on a statistical data set of in situ plasma and field measurements performed in the magnetosheath by Cluster spacecraft [ Escoubet et al. , 2001]. The data set used in this study covers two years of magnetosheath observations. All intervals when Cluster 1 spacecraft was located in the magnetosheath between dates 4 November 2006 and 10 June 2008 are included. These two years were chosen because of a favorable Cluster orbit: during this interval, the apogee was drifting south of the ecliptic plane and the orbital planewasrotatingtowardequatorialorientationresultinginagoodcoverageofthesubsolarmagnetosheath. Magnetosheath interval selection was performed by interactive visual identification of bow shock and magnetosheath crossings based on magnetic field magnitude and ion energy spectra. Two Cluster data sets were used in this study-magnetic field intensity sampled at five vectors per second from the FGM magnetometer [ Balogh et al. , 2001] and onboard calculated ion moments (density, flow velocity, and perpendicular and parallel temperatures) from the Cluster Ion Spectrometry-Hot Ion Analyzer ion spectrometer [ Rème et al. , 1997]. This instrument does not separate individual ion species; therefore, the obtained ion moments are an approximation of H + contaminated by the presence of He ++ . In a majority of cases, the He ++ abundance is low and the moments can be considered a good approximation of H + moments. A study dedicated to the He ++ population is beyond the scope of this paper, but it would represent a valuable complement to our results. All Cluster data were obtained from the public Cluster Science Archive.

The magnetosheath data were split in 5 min long intervals, and a set of field and plasma parameters was calculated on each 5 min interval. This length of interval was chosen with regard to the characteristic scale of mirror and ion cyclotron waves whose frequencies are typically between 0.05 Hz and 1 Hz. For each interval, wecalculatedaveragevaluesofionmoments,fieldmagnitude,andderivedquantitiessuchasionbetaparameter and temperature anisotropy. Intervals with missing data, low-quality data, and intervals where the CIS instrument was not in a mode appropriate for magnetosheath plasma measurements were removed. Unless specified otherwise, all data presented in this paper are calculated as averages over 5 min intervals and one 5 min interval constitutes one data point in the statistics. This way we obtained a set of 26,948 data points. The distribution of the data points in GSE coordinates with respect to average magnetosheath boundaries are shown in Figure 1. The plot demonstrates a good coverage of dayside magnetosheath near the equatorial plane and in the Southern Hemisphere.

Each interval was scanned for the presence of ULF waves using an automated technique based on variance analysis of magnetic field. Previous research discussed in section 1 has shown that two wave modes are responsible for a large majority of magnetosheath ULF waves: the mirror mode waves and Alfvén-ion cyclotron waves. In observed magnetic field data, one can distinguish between these two modes relatively easily based on their different polarization [ Krauss-Varban et al. , 1994; Song et al. , 1994]. Magnetic field variations corresponding to mirror waves are typically linearly polarized with the variation /u1D6FF B nearly parallel to background field B . Ion cyclotron waves, on the other hand, are known to be transversely left-hand polarized with the k vector directed obliquely or nearly parallel to the background field [ Remya et al. , 2014].

Each interval of data is analyzed using standard variance analysis [ Sonnerup and Sheible , 1998] to obtain the minimum and maximum variance direction vectors. We also transform the magnetic field waveform to field-aligned coordinates (using mean field vector ̄ B to obtain RMS values of fluctuations in ̄ B parallel and perpendicular direction: /u1D6FF B 2 ‖ and /u1D6FF B 2 ⊥ . We now classify as mirror events those intervals, where /u1D6FF B 2 ‖ ∕ ̄ B 2 &gt; 0 . 1, /u1D6FF B 2 ‖ ∕ /u1D6FF B 2 ⊥ &gt; 2, and the angle between ̄ B and the maximum variance direction is less than 25 ∘ . This criterion is similar to that used, for example, by Génot et al. [2009b]. Analogously, intervals are identified as AIC events when /u1D6FF B 2 ⊥ ∕ ̄ B 2 &gt; 0 . 1, /u1D6FF B 2 ‖ ∕ /u1D6FF B 2 ⊥ &lt; 0 . 5, and the angle between ̄ B and the minimum variance direction is less than 45 ∘ . The mirror intervals are further classified as mirror peaks or mirror dips based on the value of normalized skewness S of the magnetic field magnitude | B | [ Génot et al. , 2009b], providing a simple and easily reproducible criterion. Mirror intervals where S &gt; 0 . 1 are classified as peaks and intervals where S &lt; -0 . 1 as dips.

It should be noted that this simple method suitable for large-scale studies does not identify other transverse wave modes that might be present (such as the fast MHD mode), and these are classified as AIC waves. Mixed events containing a signature of both mirror and AIC waves are not resolved either.

## 2.1. Estimating Position in the Magnetosheath and Corresponding Bow Shock Parameters

One of the objectives of our study is to relate the local properties of waves and plasmas to the parameters of the bow shock. Previous studies discussed above in section 1 have shown that a significant dawn-dusk asymmetry exists in magnetosheath parameters and waves and that it is most likely due to the asymmetry introduced by IMF orientation and the location of Q ⊥ and Q ‖ shocks.

Clearly, the local parameters of the shock, in particular the angle Θ Bn , vary very strongly with the location on the bow shock surface. In this paper we take a direct approach to the problem and attempt to derive for each magnetosheath observation the parameters of the bow shock at the location and time where the observedplasmaelemententeredthemagnetosheath.Ourmethodemploysbacktracingthemagnetosheath plasma flow line from the observation point to the shock to obtain the probable entry point of the observed plasma into the magnetosheath. A similar approach was employed previously for tracing the evolution of mirror structures [ Tátrallyay et al. , 2008; Génot et al. , 2011], for magnetosheath ion fluxes [ Hayosh et al. , 2005] and magnetic field oscillations [ Luhmannet al. , 1986; Shevyrev and Zastenker , 2005].

To perform this tracing procedure, we used a magnetosheath flow model described and validated in Soucek and Escoubet [2012]. In the present study we also used the same dynamic bow shock and magnetopause models with OMNI2 data set providing the upstream solar wind parameters: the Farris et al. [1991] model for the bow shock and Shue et al. [1997] for the magnetopause. Correction for aberration of solar wind by the orbital motion of the Earth was consistently applied.

This flow line tracing was performed for every point in the data set, and for each point we obtained the corresponding point of origin on the model bow shock and the time required for the observed plasma element to be convected from the shock to the spacecraft location. Using those two parameters and the same Farris et al. [1991] bow shock model, we then calculated the local parameters of the source shock: the Alfvén Mach number MA and the angle Θ Bn between model shock normal and IMF. ## 3.1. Dependence of Plasma Stability and Wave Occurrence on Bow Shock Parameters

The dependence of the investigated plasma parameters on the properties of the upstream shock crossing is shown in Figure 4. Color coded are median values of A , ion /u1D6FD ‖ , and R calculated from data points binned by Θ Bn and MA . The strong dependence of all three plasma parameters on the shock properties is evident: the strongest anisotropy is observed at quasi-perpendicular shocks with low Mach number, while quasi-parallel shocks with large Mach number are correlated with the highest values of /u1D6FD ‖ . The observed anisotropy-beta anticorrelation can be attributed to the double adiabatic fluid theory Chew-Goldberger-Low (CGL) law which predicts an inverse dependence between A and /u1D6FD ‖ during adiabatic plasma compression or expansion. CGL lawcanbeappliedtomagnetosheathplasmaexpansionontheglobalscale,butbothmirrorandioncyclotron instabilities are known to constrain this relationship by dissipating excess anisotropy [ Anderson and Fuselier , 1993; Fuselier et al. , 1994].

Figure 4d shows the number of values in each bin used to calculate the medians and demonstrates a good statistical coverage with the exception of rare nearly parallel high Mach number shocks. The mirror instability parameter R indicates that the most unstable conditions occur for quasi-perpendicular high Mach number shocks. These dependencies are qualitatively consistent with the trends calculated from anisotropic Rankine-Hugoniot conditions [ Génot , 2009; Chaoet al. , 1995], but a direct comparison to the theory is impossible without additional information on the shock properties not available in our model. Later in this article we show that other factors contribute to this statistics, but the dominant trends are those presented here.

In Figure 5 is presented a statistics of ULF wave occurrence as a function of the same shock parameters as in Figure 4. This figure needs to be interpreted together with Figure 4 as the wave occurrence depends strongly on the average plasma parameters. This can be interpreted in terms of competition between mirror and IC instabilities. AIC waves only appear when mirror instability is inhibited. This can suggest that when linear theory predicts a positive growth rate for both instabilities, the mirror instability dominates despite the fact that the growth rate of ion cyclotron waves should be larger. In the case when mirror instability threshold is not reached, the AIC waves can grow uninhibited.

A convenient explanation is provided by the different saturation properties of the instabilities as demonstrated in three-dimensional hybrid simulations by Shoji et al. [2009]. In their simulation, the authors follow the competition of the two instabilities from initial linear stage to nonlinear saturation. It is shown that while initially, the ion cyclotron stability grows faster, the growth is quickly saturated and the AIC waves are damped after a short time. The mirror waves, on the other hand, while initially growing slower, reach a stable saturated state in which large-amplitude | B | oscillations persist for a long time. This mechanism explains this feature and also the large dominance of mirror modes over AIC waves in magnetosheath observations.

Asimilar effect has been analyzed by Russell et al. [1999] in their study of Galileo observations of ion cyclotron and mirror waves in the wake of Io. In their data set, the ion cyclotron waves at SO + 2 gyrofrequency were observed during the Io approach, but two narrow bands of mirror wave activity were observed at the edges of the wake. ## 4. Conclusions

In this paper we have studied a statistical distribution of plasma parameters relevant to mirror and ion cyclotron instabilities as well as the distribution of mirror and AIC waves themselves in the terrestrial magnetosheath. Magnetosheath flow model [ Soucek and Escoubet , 2012] was employed in the calculation of the relative location of the observation with respect to magnetosheath boundaries. The following main conclusions were reached from the statistical data:

1. Values of A , R , and /u1D6FD ‖ depend strongly on the parameters of the upstream shock crossing (Figure 4). High Mach number Q ⊥ shocks result in the largest value of R , low Mach number Q ⊥ shocks correspond to low values of /u1D6FD ‖ and high anisotropy. Highest values of /u1D6FD ‖ and low anisotropy are associated with high Mach number Q ‖ shocks.
2. Strongest temperature anisotropy A and very low /u1D6FD ‖ are found in the subsolar region near the magnetopause where PDL is formed. In the flank magnetosheath, /u1D6FD ‖ low is correlated with moderate anisotropy. The most mirror unstable plasma is found in subsolar magnetosheath close to the bow shock.
3. Mirror waves occur in Q ⊥ magnetosheath and are much more common in correlation with high Mach number shock.
